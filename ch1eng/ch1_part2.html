Part 2: Nature of Matter & General Classification

Anything that has Mass and occupies Space (volume) is called Matter.

Examples: Pen, pencil, fan, air, living beings, water, etc.

2.1 Classification Based on Physical State:

i) Solid

A state in which constituent particles are held very close to each other due to strong Intermolecular Forces.

  • Particles are extremely close to each other.
  • Intermolecular distance is minimal.
  • Melting and Boiling Points are high.
  • Intermolecular force of attraction is very strong.
  • Thermal Energy is the lowest.
  • Both shape and volume are definite.

ii) Liquid

A state where constituent particles are slightly farther apart compared to solids, but closer than in gases.

  • Intermolecular distance is greater than solids but less than gases.
  • Intermolecular force is weaker than solids but stronger than gases.
  • Thermal Energy is higher than solids but lower than gases.
  • Shape is indefinite (takes shape of container), but volume is definite.

iii) Gases

A state where constituent particles are very far apart from each other.

  • Intermolecular distance is maximum.
  • Intermolecular force of attraction is negligible.
  • Both shape and volume are indefinite.
  • Thermal Energy is maximum.

1. Pure Substances

Substances that contain only ONE type of particles.

  • Elements: The simplest form of matter which cannot be broken down chemically. Ex: H, He, C, Au, Ag.
  • Compounds: Formed when atoms of two or more elements combine in a fixed ratio. Ex: NH₃, H₂O, CO₂.

2. Mixtures

Substances where two or more components are present in ANY ratio.

  • Homogeneous Mixture: Components are completely mixed and uniformly distributed. Ex: Sugar solution in water.
  • Heterogeneous Mixture: Components are not uniformly mixed and remain segregated. Ex: Sand and salt, Oil and water.
