Part 11: Stoichiometry & Limiting Reagent

11.1 Chemical Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry deals with the calculation of masses (or volumes) of reactants and products involved in a chemical reaction. The word comes from Greek stoicheion (element) and metron (measure).

💡 4 Rules to Solve Stoichiometry Problems:

1Write the **Balanced Chemical Equation**.
2Convert given mass/volume into **Moles**.
3Use the **Coefficients** ratio to find moles of the unknown substance.
4Convert those moles back into mass or volume.

Example: Combustion of Methane

CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) ⟶ CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g)

Question: How much water (in grams) is produced by the combustion of 16g of methane?

Solution:
1. 16g CH₄ = 1 mole.
2. According to equation, 1 mole CH₄ gives 2 moles H₂O.
3. Mass of 1 mole H₂O = 18g.
4. Mass of 2 moles H₂O = 2 × 18 = **36g**.

11.2 Limiting Reagent

🥪 The Sandwich Analogy

If you need 2 slices of bread + 1 slice of cheese to make 1 sandwich:
If you have **10 bread slices** (can make 5 sandwiches) but only **2 cheese slices**, you can only make 2 sandwiches.
The **Cheese** is the **Limiting Reagent** because it runs out first and limits the production.

Definition: The reactant that is completely consumed first in a reaction is called the **Limiting Reagent**. It determines the amount of product formed.

Example: 2 moles H₂ + 2 moles O₂

2H₂(g) + O₂(g) ⟶ 2H₂O(l)

According to the equation, 2 moles of H₂ require only 1 mole of O₂.
If we start with 2 moles of each, the H₂ will be completely used up, while 1 mole of O₂ will remain unreacted.
H₂ is the Limiting Reagent.
