Laws of Chemical Combinations

Part 6: Laws of Chemical Combinations

The combination of elements to form compounds is governed by five basic laws of chemical combinations:

1. Law of Conservation of Mass

🔹 Proposed by Antoine Lavoisier (1789).

"Matter can neither be created nor destroyed. In a chemical reaction, the total mass of reactants is equal to the total mass of products."
Example: Combustion of Carbon
C + O₂ → CO₂
12g + 32g → 44g
(Mass of Reactants = 44g | Mass of Product = 44g)

2. Law of Definite Proportions

🔹 Proposed by Joseph Proust.

"A given compound always contains exactly the same proportion of elements by weight, regardless of its source."
Example: Water (H₂O)
A sample of pure water, whether from a well or a river, always contains Hydrogen and Oxygen in the mass ratio of 1:8.

3. Law of Multiple Proportions

🔹 Proposed by John Dalton (1803).

"If two elements can combine to form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other element are in the ratio of small whole numbers."
Example: Carbon and Oxygen
• In CO, 12g Carbon combines with 16g Oxygen.
• In CO₂, 12g Carbon combines with 32g Oxygen.
Ratio of Oxygen masses = 16:32 = 1:2.

4. Gay Lussac’s Law of Gaseous Volumes

🔹 Proposed by Gay Lussac (1808).

"When gases combine or are produced in a chemical reaction, they do so in a simple ratio by volume, provided all gases are at the same temperature and pressure."
Example: Hydrogen + Oxygen → Steam
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(g)
(2 vol) + (1 vol) → (2 vol)
Ratio = 2 : 1 : 2

5. Avogadro’s Law

🔹 Proposed by Amedeo Avogadro (1811).

"Equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure should contain equal number of molecules."
